# Atlas of AI by Kate Crawford — BS-Scored Summary

**Author:** Kate Crawford (2021)
**Topics:** Labor exploitation, environmental cost, classification bias
**Overall BS Score:** 3.2/10
**Verdict:** The most well-evidenced AI critique — material analysis backed by investigative reporting

## Overview

Atlas of AI is the anti-hype book. Where most AI books discuss algorithms and capabilities, Crawford examines the physical infrastructure: the mines where rare earth minerals are extracted, the warehouses where data is labeled by low-wage workers, the server farms consuming enormous energy. Every claim is backed by investigative reporting, site visits, and documentary evidence.

## Key Claims

| Claim | BS Score | Rating | Verdict |
|-------|----------|--------|---------|
| AI is built on exploited labor, extracted resources, and biased data | ~2.8 | Verified | Well-documented with specific examples, confirmed by subsequent reporting |
| Environmental cost of AI is systematically understated | ~3.0 | Verified | Confirmed by Luccioni et al energy studies and industry emissions data |
| Classification systems reproduce social hierarchies | ~3.5 | Mixed | Strong evidence for bias, "reproduce hierarchies" is slightly overstated |

## Deep Dive: The Labor Behind AI

Crawford's most powerful chapter examines Amazon Mechanical Turk workers, content moderators, and data labelers. These workers — often in the Global South — create the training data that powers AI systems, typically for pennies per task. The analysis is specific, documented, and has been confirmed by subsequent investigative journalism (Time Magazine's report on OpenAI's Kenyan content moderators).

**Evidence quality: 8/10.** Crawford visited facilities, interviewed workers, cited compensation data. This is journalism-grade evidence, not speculation.

## The Crawford BS Pattern

Crawford's weakness is occasional rhetorical overreach. "AI is neither artificial nor intelligent" is a provocative framing that goes slightly beyond the evidence. AI IS artificial in the engineering sense, and it IS intelligent in the narrow task sense. The material critique is strong; the philosophical framing occasionally overshoots.

## Who Should Read This

Essential for anyone who thinks AI is "just software." Crawford's material analysis is a permanent corrective to the industry's tendency to ignore the physical and human infrastructure that makes AI possible.
